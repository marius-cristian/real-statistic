Real purchasing power: Romania, France, Germany

A basket-based experiment testing whether the Eurostat "Romania is at 78% of EU average" narrative matches what a median net salary actually buys.

Where reality deviates from the EU statistic

Eurostat publishes one number: GDP per capita in PPS, EU27=100. Romania sits at 77. This project recomputes real purchasing power per basket, and the answer changes depending on what you buy.

Positive = PPS flatters that country for that basket. Negative = PPS understates. For global (tradables like iPhones, laptops, flights) the PPS illusion is at its worst: prices barely vary across the EU, so the "cheap country" adjustment collapses.

"Foarte important: asta NU înseamnă salariul mediu și nici câți bani are fiecare român în buzunar."
A common framing of the PPS finding. The disclaimer is correct. The question: how far does the PPS number drift from lived reality once we pick an explicit basket?
Caveats the numbers cannot fix.
  • Capital-density premium. Paris rent is about 2× the French national average per m². Berlin sits closer to the German urban average. Bucharest is close to the Romanian national rent average. So housing and middle_class baskets exaggerate Romania's housing gap somewhat.
  • Amenity value. Paris (and Berlin, and Bucharest in its own way) deliver cultural density, gastronomy, landmarks, luxury retail, and scene access that baskets cannot price. Part of what Parisians pay for rent is proximity to all of that, not pure cost-of-living. The same applies everywhere in degrees.
  • Clean read. The global basket (iPhone, laptop, flights, streaming) contains no rent and no amenities, only tradables. That is where the PPS narrative is most cleanly testable.

8 · Reality vs the EU statistic

The 77% figure is real, and the pipeline reproduces it from raw Eurostat inputs within ±1 pp. The dispute is not with the math. It is with the interpretation.

  1. For tradables, PPS is misleading. A Romanian median net buys fewer iPhones, laptops, or EU flights per month than a German or French one, by the ratio of their salaries, because tradables cost almost the same across the EU. The PPS "cheap country" adjustment evaporates as soon as the basket contains anything traded. Our global basket shows a divergence of roughly +29 pp for RO and +33 pp for FR; both countries are flattered by PPS when it comes to goods shipped in from abroad.
  2. For a middle-class lifestyle, PPS is close but slightly optimistic. Rent, car, groceries, and some services come out near the PPS number for RO (about +2 pp). Not a scandal, but not the "78% of EU average lifestyle" either. It is 78% of a heavily domestic-biased basket.
  3. Paris distorts the French picture. Housing-only divergence is +50 pp for FR, because Paris rent is not a French-national number. It is a global-city premium. A similar effect would appear for London or Zurich. It does not appear for Berlin or Bucharest to the same degree.
  4. Housing in Bucharest is genuinely cheap. About 9 pp of understatement on housing for RO, the only place PPS understates Romanian purchasing power. This is why PPS looks good for RO overall: the housing tailwind compensates for the tradables headwind in the aggregate.

The common caveat ("nu înseamnă salariul mediu și nici câți bani are fiecare român în buzunar") is literally true. What the data shows is stronger. The PPS number is correctly computed, but it averages across basket types with very different reality-checks. Telling a Romanian shopping for a laptop that they are "at 78% of EU average" is not supported by what that laptop actually costs them in hours worked.

9 · Methodology and sources

Formulas 
basket_cost_local[b, pc] = Sum of price[i, pc] * quantity[i, b]
basket_cost_eur[b, pc]   = basket_cost_local[b, pc] * fx[pc -> EUR]
net_eur[c]               = net[c] * fx[c -> EUR]
bpm[b, sc, pc]           = net_eur[sc] / basket_cost_eur[b, pc]
rpp[b, sc, pc]           = 100 * bpm[b, sc, pc] / bpm[middle_class, DE, DE]
divergence[b, c]         = official_index[c] - rebased_bpm[b, c, c]
cross_index[b, sc, pc]   = 100 * bpm[b, sc, pc] / bpm[b, pc, pc]
