a real wallet test

"Romania is at 78% of the EU average." Fine. What does that buy you?

What happens when you take one person's median salary and shop for real things (rent, iPhones, groceries, flights) at real store prices in Romania, France, and Germany.

One gadget, three different months

How many months of your salary, after you have paid for a basic life (rent, food, utilities, transit), you need to save up for one PS5, one iPhone 15, or one MacBook Air. Same product. Same price within a few euros. Very different months of life.

Months of post-survival leftover needed to buy one unit, at local prices. Lower is better. "Survival" = rent + groceries + transit + utilities + phone, at each country's capital prices.

A Romanian saves for two to four months of their entire post-survival income to buy what a German pays for in half a month to two weeks. Eurostat says Romania is at 77% of the EU average. Measured against an iPhone, it is closer to 29%. The number is not lying. It is just not answering the question you were asking.

What we're testing, and why

The claim under test. A widely quoted talking point says Romania sits at 78% of the EU average in "GDP per capita at Purchasing Power Standards" (PPS), so "things are better than they feel". The 77% figure is real. Eurostat publishes it. It is not a lie.

Our hypothesis. That number is also not the whole truth. PPS is a weighted average of prices across an entire economy, heavily tilted toward rent, domestic services, and groceries. Cross-border goods (an iPhone, a MacBook, a plane ticket to Berlin) cost nearly the same in every EU country, so the "cheap country" adjustment that PPS applies barely moves them. The discount only works for things that stay inside the country.

If we go item by item at real store prices, does "78% of EU average" hold for the stuff people actually buy, or does it split into different answers for different baskets?

What we did, step by step

  1. Built four shopping lists: survival (rent, food, transport), middle-class (add car, gym, vacations), global (iPhones, laptops, streaming, a flight), and housing-only. Same list applied to every country.
  2. Priced every item at a real store. Carrefour for groceries, Apple for phones, SeLoger and ImmoScout24 for rent, national transit authorities for monthly passes, carrier sites for phone plans. Every row has a clickable link to the source.
  3. Pulled median net salary per country from the statistical office, converted everything to euros at the ECB reference rate.
  4. Divided: salary over basket cost = baskets per month. Ran this for every combination, RO salary on RO prices, RO salary on FR prices, DE salary on RO prices, all nine crosses, for all four baskets.
  5. Reproduced Eurostat's own PPS calculation from their GDP and population inputs first, to prove the math checks out. Our reproduction matches the official index within ±1 percentage point. Then compared the per-basket answers against the single 77% headline.

Why this is more grounded than the PPS number

PPS takes Eurostat's survey of about 2,400 goods and services, blends them into a single national price level, and uses that one number to adjust GDP per capita. A lot of adjustment rides on one blended input, and most of what goes into it is services and housing: categories Romanians do consume locally. So the adjustment works for those.

This project does not hide anything in a single blended number. You see:

  • What is in the basket (an explicit list of items).
  • What each item costs (a store URL).
  • What each country's median earner takes home (a stats-office URL).
  • The formula: salary over basket cost. No weights, no deflators.

If you disagree with the basket, change the basket. The YAML files are open. If you think a price is wrong, click the link and check. PPS takes all of that on trust. This project does not.

PPS is not wrong. It is too compressed for questions like "can I afford a laptop?" or "can I live in Paris?" that the basket check answers directly. This work complements PPS rather than replacing it. It is the part PPS averages away.

What people actually earn

Take-home per month, after tax and social contributions. Both medians (the middle earner, with half above and half below) and means (the arithmetic average, skewed up by top earners). The median is the honest "typical earner" number. The mean is what headlines usually quote. All the basket math below uses medians.

Median net (the typical earner)

Mean net (the average, skewed by top earners)

Means and medians agree on the gap: Germans and French earn roughly 2.5 to 3 times what Romanians earn. Medians are lower than means everywhere because the top decile pulls the average up, and that decile is not the person this analysis is about.

The PPS statistic corrects for prices: "Romanians earn less but things are cheaper there, so it evens out." The question is what exactly it evens out for.

A basket you can actually hold

Four explicit shopping lists, each priced at a real retailer whose URL is linked from the technical page:

  • Survival basket. A small apartment, groceries, public transport, utilities, phone.
  • Middle-class basket. Bigger apartment, car, groceries with some brand items, gym, dining out, one vacation a year.
  • Global basket. iPhone, laptop, streaming, an EU flight. Items whose price barely changes between countries.
  • Housing only. Rent, utilities, and the long-run cost of buying a flat in the capital.

Now imagine shopping abroad

Bucharest is cheap. Paris is expensive. A German on a German salary shopping in Bucharest affords more than twice the middle-class baskets a Parisian affords in Paris.

Global stuff costs the same

An iPhone in Bucharest and an iPhone in Berlin cost within a few euros of each other. Same for a MacBook, Netflix, a flight to Frankfurt. These are tradable goods, and their prices do not bend to local wages.

When PPS says Romania is "78% of EU average" and implies Romanians have 78% of the purchasing power, it is only telling you that about domestic stuff (bread, rent, transport). The moment you want something from Apple, Amazon, or a Wizz Air flight, the 78% shrinks considerably.

Global basket: only tradables (iPhone, laptop, streaming, a flight)

Nearly identical basket prices in all three countries (within 30%). Very different salaries. Romanian purchasing power for these goods is about — of German purchasing power, well below what the headline "77" would predict.

And the flip side: Paris is expensive even for Parisians

Paris rent is about twice the French national average per square meter. It is a global city with a global-city premium. Comparing Bucharest rent to Paris rent, capital to capital, exaggerates Romania's housing disadvantage, because Paris rent is extreme even by French standards.

Berlin sits in the middle. Bucharest is close to Romania's national average rent because Romania has no other comparably large city.

Housing-only basket: rent, utilities, buying a 60m² flat

Romanian housing is genuinely cheap, even after adjusting for salary. The French "disadvantage" on this line is largely about Paris, not France as a whole.

One thing the basket cannot count: Paris, Berlin, and Bucharest all carry cultural weight (museums, music, food, landmarks, luxury retail). Part of what Parisians pay in rent is proximity to all of it. The basket method sees pure cost; in reality some of that cost is also value. The same applies in every capital, in degrees.

The honest answer

The "77%" figure is correctly computed. This project's pipeline reproduces it from Eurostat's raw inputs within ±1 percentage point. The math checks. What misleads is how the number is quoted in public: as a single summary of "how well Romanians live compared to Europeans." That is not what it measures.

Breaking the same question into baskets splits the one 77 into four very different numbers:

  • 72% Survival basket (rent, groceries, transit, utilities). A Romanian median salary at Romanian prices buys 72% of what a German median salary buys in Germany for the same life. PPS would predict 67%. So PPS is about right here, even slightly pessimistic.
  • 71% Middle-class lifestyle. Also in line with PPS. For "can a Romanian live a local middle-class life at home?", PPS gives a fair answer.
  • 46% Global goods (iPhone, MacBook, streaming, an EU flight). A Romanian median salary buys less than half of what a German one buys on these, because the goods cost nearly the same everywhere. PPS predicts 67%. Reality is 21 percentage points worse. Biggest miss.
  • 82% Housing in the capital (rent, utilities, long-run cost of buying a flat). Bucharest really is cheap. PPS predicts 67%; reality is 15 percentage points better.

Why does this texture disappear in PPS? Because Eurostat's methodology weights the basket heavily toward things that stay inside the country (housing, local services, a slice of food). Those are cheap in Romania. The weighting makes Romania look closer to the European average than it feels when you open the Apple store's website.

The headline finding

Survival and middle-class: PPS is fair. Global (tradables): PPS overstates Romanian purchasing power by 21 pp. Housing in the capital: PPS understates Romanian purchasing power by 15 pp. One number, four realities.

Why the basket approach is more grounded

Not because basket methods are perfect. They have their own biases: they cannot price amenities like Paris's cultural density, they use capital-city rent which exaggerates the French gap, and they ignore the gap between medians and means. The methodology doc flags all of that.

The approach is grounded in things you can check. You can click the Carrefour link and see the pasta price. You can click the Apple link and see the iPhone price. You can open the Eurostat nama_10_gdp URL and see the GDP. You cannot do any of that with "78%"; it is the output of a multi-step calculation whose inputs are themselves weighted aggregates.

PPS answers: "on average, relative to all of Europe, how rich is country X?" This project answers: "at today's prices, what does a median salary buy at a real checkout?" Both are valid questions. Only the second one lets you click the answer.

What this does not prove

Not a claim that Romania is poor, backward, or "last in Europe." The housing numbers show Romania is doing better than the headline suggests. Not a claim that Paris is a bad place to live: Paris's cost reflects cultural density, opportunity, and amenities our baskets cannot count, and Parisians get real value for their higher rent.

What the data shows: one summary statistic, used too broadly, misleads in both directions. Romanian life is not 78% of a German's. It is ~100% on groceries, ~40% on gadgets, and ~110% on capital rent (relative to what PPS predicts). Pick the basket that matches your question.

Methodology & data

Everything is open. Every number has a source:

  • Technical page: formulas, per-basket PPS deviation chart, full cross-country matrix, per-country price tables, reproduction of Eurostat's index within ±1 pp.
  • GitHub: marius-cristian/real-statistic. Code, price YAMLs, written methodology (METHODOLOGY.md), bibliography (SOURCES.md).
  • Raw data files, citable line by line: macro.yaml, salaries.yaml, baskets.yaml, data/prices/.

Want to change the basket, add items, or verify prices? Clone the repo, edit the YAML files, run make build. Everything regenerates from the data.
